## The problem with the playbook

Every social platform uses the same engagement toolkit: push notifications to drive opens, infinite scroll to extend sessions, social proof to create FOMO, friction-free sharing to grow the network. For neurotypical users, these patterns work. For neurodivergent users, they're a nervous system attack.

Hiki's audience was autistic and ADHD adults. The standard playbook wasn't just ineffective — it was harmful. Push notifications trigger anxiety in populations with rejection sensitivity. Infinite scroll exploits dopamine dysregulation. Social proof creates masking pressure in communities trying to unmask.

<PullQuote cite="Design principle">The engagement playbook optimizes for time-on-app. We needed to optimize for safety-to-return.</PullQuote>

## The reframe

I didn't start by bolt-on accessibility modifications to an existing social app template. That approach — take a standard app, add accommodations — treats neurodivergent needs as edge cases. The architecture has to change.

The question wasn't "how do we make a social app accessible?" It was "what does a social platform look like when the nervous system is the primary design constraint?"

Four principles emerged:

## Notifications are opt-in

No push notifications by default. Users choose what they want to be notified about, and can set quiet hours. The onboarding flow makes this explicit: "You control when this app talks to you."

## Escape routes everywhere

Every screen has a clear, visible exit. No modal traps. No "are you sure?" friction on leaving. If a conversation gets overwhelming, you can leave without social penalty — the other person sees "stepped away," not "left."

## Spaces for unmasking

Group spaces are labeled by energy level and communication style, not just topic. "Low-energy text only" is a valid room. "Voice chat, cameras optional" is another. Users can filter by sensory environment before entering.

## Sensory defaults

Default state is calm. Animations are minimal and respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Sound is off. Contrast meets WCAG AAA, not just AA. The app doesn't compete for attention — it holds space.

## The personal dimension

Most accessibility work starts with research. I started with lived data. I'm AuDHD — the intersection of autistic and ADHD neurotypes. I wasn't designing for an abstract user persona. I was designing for a nervous system I understand from the inside.

That's not a sentimental point. It's a methodological one. Lived experience of sensory overwhelm, rejection sensitivity, and executive function challenges informed design decisions that user research alone wouldn't surface — because users often can't articulate what their nervous system needs until they experience the alternative.

## Why this matters beyond Hiki

Behavioral design is upstream of product design, not adjacent to it. Every interface triggers a nervous system response. Most designers don't account for that because the default user is assumed to be neurotypical, emotionally neutral, and cognitively available.

Designing for neurodivergent users forces you to design for the full range of human cognitive states. Those designs are better for everyone — they just happen to be essential for some.

The thing AI tools can't replace here: understanding that an infinite scroll isn't a layout pattern — it's a dopamine exploit. That distinction requires a model of human behavior that no design tool contains.
