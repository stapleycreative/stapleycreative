## The question every design leader is quietly asking

AI tools can now generate a full interface from a text prompt. Lovable, v0, Cursor, Magic Patterns — they're fast, they're getting better, and they're reshaping what it means to "design" a product.

So where does the designer come in?

I've spent the last year working with these tools daily — not as novelties, but as core design materials. Building prototypes with Claude, generating component libraries with v0, shipping interactive artifacts that would have taken a team two weeks to produce.

Here's what I've learned: the tools are extraordinary at execution. And they are consistently terrible at three things.

## Diagnosis

Every project starts with a stated problem. "The form is too slow." "Users aren't converting." "The dashboard is cluttered."

The stated problem is almost never the real problem.

At GiveCampus, everyone assumed the contact report form needed to be faster. Fifteen fields, too much clicking. The obvious fix: streamline the form. But the real problem wasn't speed — it was that the form discarded the meaning of donor relationships. Gift officers filed reports because compliance required it, not because the tool helped them think.

That reframe — seeing the real problem underneath the stated problem — changed everything about the design direction. No AI tool would have surfaced it. Not because the tools aren't smart, but because diagnosis requires context that lives outside the interface: organizational dynamics, domain expertise, watching users struggle with things they can't articulate.

## Behavioral judgment

An AI tool can generate a notification system. It cannot know that the notification system should be opt-in by default because your users have rejection sensitivity and surprise stimuli trigger fight-or-flight responses.

When I designed Hiki — a social platform for neurodivergent adults — the standard engagement playbook (push notifications, infinite scroll, social proof) wasn't just ineffective. It was actively harmful. An infinite scroll isn't a neutral layout pattern. It's a dopamine exploit. Knowing the difference requires a model of human behavior that no design tool contains.

This isn't niche. Every interface triggers a nervous system response. Most designers don't account for it because the default user is assumed to be neurotypical, emotionally neutral, and cognitively available. That assumption is wrong for a significant portion of your users — and designing for the full range makes products better for everyone.

## Architectural taste

At iFIT, I had to decide: build shared components across five product surfaces, or build a shared token layer and let each surface compose its own layouts? Both approaches look reasonable on paper. One of them creates compounding consistency over three years. The other creates compounding fragmentation.

That decision — tokens first, components second — was invisible for the first two weeks. No UI to show. But every component built afterward was automatically consistent across treadmill, bike, rower, app, and web. The alternative (build components, extract tokens later) would have created the exact fragmentation the system was designed to prevent.

AI tools can build either approach. They cannot tell you which one to choose. That judgment comes from watching systems succeed and fail over time, from understanding which decisions compound and which decisions don't matter. There is no shortcut for that kind of taste.

## The designer's real job

The designer's job isn't being replaced by AI. It's being clarified by AI. When execution is cheap, the premium shifts to the things that were always valuable but hard to see: diagnosis, behavioral judgment, and architectural taste.

The designers who thrive in this landscape won't be the ones who resist the tools or the ones who become tool operators. They'll be the ones who use AI to amplify the judgment that only comes from years of watching humans interact with systems — and who can articulate why that judgment matters.

That's the work I do. The tools have changed. The work hasn't.
